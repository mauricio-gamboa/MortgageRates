Closing Costs: Why They Add Up Fast

By ClosingCosts.ca on August 2, 2013 No comments

When it comes to home ownership, first-time buyers often forget to budget for their closing costs. Whether you're buying, selling or refinancing a property, closing costs can add up fast. How fast? Deposit

When you make an offer on a home, you'll need to include a deposit. A deposit demonstrates to the buyer that you are serious about purchasing the property. The size of your deposit depends on the location of the home - it's often 1% of the purchase price, but can be as much as 5% in cities like Toronto where bidding wars exist.

Home Inspection

Buying a home is likely the biggest investment you'll ever make - as such, one optional closing cost that you shouldn't skip out on is a home inspection. A home inspector's job is to examine the interior and exterior condition of the home and give you a report on its condition. If everything looks good, you'll feel better about moving forward with the purchase. You should set aside at least $500 for a home inspection.

CMHC Insurance

If you make a down payment of less than 20%, your mortgage is considered to be high-ratio and you will need to purchase mortgage default insurance for the loan. More commonly known as CMHC insurance, mortgage default insurance is added to your mortgage loan and paid out over the life of your mortgage. In Manitoba, Ontario and Quebec, you'll also have to pay PST on your premium and that amount is due in cash at closing.

Land Transfer Tax

Whenever you buy real estate, you will have to pay a land transfer tax. Unlike Alberta and Saskatchewan, who only charge a small fee, most provinces calculate it as a percentage of your home price and the amount you owe is due in cash at closing. Land transfer tax is often the most expensive closing cost you'll have to pay for and can easily add up to thousands of dollars. And if you're in Toronto, you'll have to pay both a provincial land transfer tax and an additional municipal land transfer tax.

Legal Fees

You may not know it now but your real estate lawyer will be your silent hero throughout your real estate transaction. Your lawyer's job is to protect you during the whole process: from reviewing your Agreement of Purchase and Sale, to doing a title search, making your payments and everything in between. You should set aside at least $1,500 for legal fees and disbursements.

Additional Closing Costs

On top of these common closing costs, you may have to pay for others, including: GST or HST on a new home purchase, a new home warranty enrollment fee, a land survey fee, reimbursement of prepaid taxes or utilities and more. Whether you're buying, selling or refinancing, ClosingCosts.ca has built a closing costs calculator that will help you calculate how much these additional expenses could add up to. By entering in some basic details about a property, you can get a better understanding of how much you'll need to save up and budget for, so you can feel prepared for the total bill that comes on closing day.
